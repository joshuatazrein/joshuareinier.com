Aperghis' Avis de Tempête

Georges Aperghis' ambitious opera "Avis de Tempête (Storm Warning)" is aptly named. The work surges between a rotating cast of mutterers, polemicists, gibberish-mouthers, and whirling dervishes, spinning around the radio tower in the middle of the stage. Instead of a linear narrative, or even grammatical sentences, there's a pastiche of broken phrases drawn from authors including Herman Melville, Shakespeare, Biblical references, and theoretical texts commenting on the nature of storms—both inner and outer. The opera proceeds by rhythm, by contradiction. It's a text that writes itself before your eyes.

Avis isn't the only example of the French composer cutting up language. Retrouvailles, for two percussionists, deconstructs the gestures and expressions that go on in daily social interaction. The two musicians hug, then launch into polyrhythms on each other's backs, shout out spontaneously, and at one point pass a wine glass back and forth while drumming on the table. The composer has a marked interest in daily life, even from his early experiments with "flash mob"-esque performances in public settings (albeit with an avant-garde twist).

But with Avis, the composer masterfully combines his previous experiments into a full tapestry, as unified as it is surprising. The characters are voices within a single skull: they argue, yes, but they also collaborate on a tangled portrait of sounds and thoughts. And isn't this what it's like to be human, especially in today's world of distractions? Avis de tempête may not be your cup of tea; it's an unrelenting hour and forty minutes. But the opera undoubtedly speaks to the storm of society.

Event Descriptions

Raven Chacon: Pulitzer Prize-winning Composer Comes to Oberlin

​Raven Chacon is a trailblazing composer, experimental noise performer, installation artist, and educator. Resisting categorization, he draws on his Navajo origins to rethink commercialized notions of Indigenous art. Just last Saturday, Chacon won the 2022 Pulitzer Prize for "Voiceless Mass," a piece for pipe organ and chamber ensemble that considers spaces of worship and their architecture. We are excited to bring him to Oberlin today for a talk in the TIMARA Gallery at 4:30 PM and performance in Clonick Hall at 7:30 PM. The concert will feature Oberlin students performing Raven's chamber and electronic works, showcasing the breadth of his notational and stylistic palette.

Ensemble Pamplemousse: Surreal, Playful, and Brilliant

Ensemble Pamplemousse is a composer/performer collective that juxtaposes its members' diverse musical training to explore new sonic possibilities. The group's repertoire incorporates surreal storytelling, found instruments, and multimedia, twisting together disparate elements into performances which turn themselves inside out. Oberlin welcomes them this Friday, December 17, to give their first concert since the pandemic. Expect electronic recorders, music boxes, glockenspiels, and the unexpected.

Joshua Reinier presents Hauntings, a Multimedia Installation

Oberlin senior Joshua Reinier presents a ghostly experience involving dancers, spoken word, and immersive visuals. Funded by an Oberlin XARTS Grant, the piece combines elements of spoken word and sound design to present a fragmented narrative that emerges fluidly from the circulating performers. Audience members are invited to walk freely through the hall as performers repeat text fragments resembling passing conversation along with a swirling sonic tapestry. Winds blow, images coalesce and dissolve on surrounding projectors. The piece both immerses and defies, swirling as it began.

Sunday, November 11 at 7:30 PM, Wilder Main

Reinier is a vocalist, composer, and multi-instrumentalist whose searching compositions vibrate at the edge of language. His work surveys a range of styles, from poetry and electronic music to satirical theatre-pieces incorporating graphic scores and improvisation. Drawing inspiration from visionaries such as Peter Maxwell Davies and George Lewis, Reinier advances an openminded approach which sits comfortably in the expanding contemporary scene. Listeners may be reminded of performer/composers like Jennifer Walshe and Wet Ink.
